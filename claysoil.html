Clay soils feel very sticky and rolls like plasticine when wet. They can hold more total water than most other soil types and, although only about half of this is available to plants, crops seldom suffer from drought. They swell when wetted and shrink when dried, so a certain amount of restructuring can take place in these soils depending on weather conditions. They lie wet in winter and so stock should be taken off the land to avoid poaching (the compaction of soils by animals hooves). They are very late in warming up in the spring because water heats up more slowly than mineral matter. They are normally fairly rich in potash, but are deficient in phosphates. Clay soils usually need large infrequent dressings of lime. It holds water and nutrients. During dry spells, clay can still offer plants moisture.
